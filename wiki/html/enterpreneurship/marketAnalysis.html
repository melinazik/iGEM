Osteoarthritis Evolution

“Osteoarthritis” is derived from the Greek word part osteo-, meaning “bone”, combined with arthritis: arthr-, meaning “joint”, and –itis.

The disease of osteoarthritis originates from the ancient times. More specifically, the first indications of patients with the disease come from data that has survived from the Pharaonic Egypts and documentation of techniques for spinal manipulation proposed by Hippocrates and the Ancient Greeks. Also, worth mentioning is the fact that Pharaonic Egypts had a popular saying related to osteoarthritis: “Osteoarthritis of the hip for the scribes, vertebral osteoarthritis for the peasants”.

During the next aeons, doctors have spent countless hours of researching that disease. Until the 20th century few were the cases documented, but since medicine developed the cases begun to grow in numbers. More specifically, knee osteoarthritis long existed at low numbers, but since the mid-20th century, the disease has doubled in prevalence.

The doubling in numbers was the product of the development of a new diagnostic technique, the X-rays. This gave the opportunity for doctors to examine the disease at greater detail and come up with more efficient treatments for osteoarthritis.

In the past, osteoarthritis was treated by cartilage replacement. More specifically, in 1846 the replacement was done through anaesthesia, in 1865 through cochlear surgery and in 1939 through antibiotics. Nowadays osteoarthritis is treated in various invasive and non-invasive ways. The use of medicines such as paracetamol, anti-inflammatory drugs and duloxetine is essential. Because our project aims to treat knee osteoarthritis, we will now dive into the treatments concerning this specific part of our body. Non-invasive methods, apart from the abovementioned medicines, are physiotherapy, passive gymnastics with electrodes and injection therapy that includes substances such as cortisone or injections of hyaluronic acid and chondroitin into the cartilage. On the other hand, the invasive treatments consist mainly of surgical procedures. The surgical procedures are divided into two main categories: total arthroplasty (the cartilage is removed and an artificial implant is placed) and the partial one-compartment arthroplasty.





Osteoarthritis Industry Analysis

Treatments

Here in Greece, as in most countries, a patient can be treated on a public or a private hospital depending on the kind of insurance he/she/it has.


Surgery
In the case where the patient has public insurance, the cost is clearly cheaper than in the private sector. The cost varies depending on the case of osteoarthritis (total or partial orthoplasty), the type of implant but also the special CT scans. In this case, the patient being insured with EOPYY pays a specific fee (participation) for the examinations and the surgery is paid by the state. In the case of surgery in the private sector, the patient is asked to pay the difference from his insurance fund. The cost of the surgical team ranges from 1400-2500 €. However, further examinations can reach up to €900. Additional costs are the patient's room of choice (bed) and the length of the patient's stay in the hospital. In the second case of private health insurance, the entire amount (depending on the contract) of the surgery is covered by the relevant insurance company.

In most public hospitals, simple knee orthoplasty is prevalent. In private hospitals there are more innovative invasive forms, such as robotic knee orthoplasty, Navigation assistance, SuperPath Minimally Invasive Hip Arthroplasty and MIS (Minimally Invasive Method). Due to the year of economic recession in our country but also the high surgical costs in combination with the low salaries, the patients choose the surgery in the public sector. The waiting time for surgery for osteoarthritis starts at 3 months. However, the choice of surgery in the private sector is much more immediate, but costly.


Medications
Also, a patient can acquire medications that can help relieve osteoarthritis symptoms, primarily pain, with relatively low cost. The most popular choices are:


Acetaminophen: Acetaminophen (Tylenol, others) has been shown to help some people with osteoarthritis who have mild to moderate pain. Taking more than the recommended dose of acetaminophen can cause liver damage. Its cost starts from €10.

Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs): Over-the-counter NSAIDs, such as ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin IB, others) and naproxen sodium (Aleve), taken at the recommended doses, typically relieve osteoarthritis pain. Stronger NSAIDs are available by prescription. Its cost starts from €15.

Duloxetine (Cymbalta): Normally used as an antidepressant, this medication is also approved to treat chronic pain, including osteoarthritis pain. Its cost starts from €15.

Market Size

Osteoarthritis prevalence rises as the years go by. A study has shown that between the years 1990 to 2019, in European countries there is an average increase of 45% in the numbers of patients with Osteoarthritis (see the diagram below). At the same time, in a 2013 report, the World Health Organization, states: “The prevalence of OA is increasing due to population ageing and an increase in related factors such as obesity. According to the United Nations, by 2050 people aged over 60 will account for more than 20% of the world’s population. Of that 20%, a conservative estimate of 15% will have symptomatic OA, and one-third of these people will be severely disabled. This means that by 2050, 130 million people will suffer from OA worldwide, of whom 40 million will be severely disabled by the disease. Costs associated with OA include costs for adaptive aids and devices, medicines, surgery, and time off at work.




Overall, OA caused about 2 million years lived with disability (YLDs) in Western Europe in 2019, a 36% increase since 1990. Total years lived with disability attributable to OA in our focus countries in 2019 ranged from 275,405 in Germany to 25,580 in Sweden (a much less populous country). When standardised by population size, the highest rate of disability was in Spain (541 YLD per 100,000) and the lowest in Sweden (367 YLD per 100,000).
