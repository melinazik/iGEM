Handbooks

As a team we had a long, tough, exhausting but amazing and teaching iGEM journey. Learning from our mistakes and wanting to pass our epiphanies to the next person that will be in our shoes, we created two guides.

"How To iGEM"

First one is “HOW TO iGEM”, a thorough and long handbook for someone that may not yet know what the iGEM acronym stands for, but even for someone who has 2 months left for the wiki freeze. We sincerely think it fits all shapes and sizes of future participants. For that, we have to thank KOREA_HS, our high school partner team that enriched this guide with their view of point, offering insights we could never have thought of or experienced. The creation process for this guide exceeded the limits of a simple collaboration or partnership and gave both teams the opportunity to see iGEM through the eyes of someone completely different from them. It was then that we realized how similar but still unique each team’s experiences are. It was unexpected for us, some Greek university students, and a bit startling, to find out we had and exploited the chance to unite, familiarize and bond with much younger people from another peninsula!



The next guide came to be after it became pretty clear to us that we had to share our knowledge and newly gained expertise, for others to succeed along with us. We believe, irrespectively of how other people behave, that it is a duty of an iGEM team when successful in a field or task, to share their acquired knowledge. After all, that is the trait that allows the human kind to advance, in contrast to other species that do not share and build on collective knowledge. Here is the guide “How to Crowdfund”!

"How To CFR"

The economy in each country or region is for sure different from any other, and how frequent big sponsorships come along, depends on it. One thing anyone can be sure of when looking for financial support, is the willingness of people to support new ideas by young students trying to change the world in one way or another. Crowdfunding gives you that. It gives the opportunity to ordinary people to support and be part of an idea without the judgmental and precautious point of view of a big corporation.

In our iGEM experience we used crowdfunding to raise money for our registration fee, and people responded to us and helped us in our mission. We managed to raise 52% of our original target (6000$), possibly the most successful Greek iGEM crowdfunding so far. So, many Greek teams contacted us for advice on how to crowdfund. We thought, instead of spreading this self-learned experience mouth-to-mouth, to make a simple generalized guide for any country, so that any team could use our experience to furtherly succeed in their fundraising efforts. Also, we hope to spread the idea of crowdfunding, since it is not widely used within iGEM teams, and endorse the connection of a project with the society.

Here are the steps you should follow & some tips!

1) Look up crowdfunding platforms and choose those that work best in your country. If there are many, favor those that work in most countries, so that it will be easier for people from all around the world to discover and support you.

­ 2) Check the crowdfunding types each platform allows. E.g., some support only non-profit organization work or start-ups.

­ 3) Which one has the lowest fee? (Watch out! Some platforms seem to have a very low fee, though they only allow payments through PayPal so there are additional fees you didn’t see at first)

­ 4)Chose the crowdfunding platform that has the most available paying methods (you need to make it easy to the possible supporters to contribute to your cause, e.g. if you can donate only via PayPal and the supporter does not have in account, it is very possible that he/she will not end up contributing).

5)Look up your country’s financial laws. What is the limit for money to be taxed? You would like to raise less than that so that you don’t have to engage with tax paying procedures. (e.g. In Greece if you get less than 6.000$ euro, it is not taxable, so we set the target to 6.000$)

6)Crowdfunding platforms usually ask for a personal account to be registered in order to receive the money. So, someone from your team should add their personal account. Be sure to have transparency in your transactions. (e.g., we had a shared OneNote where we posted when and how much money were withdrawn, with notes and screenshots)

7)Write a descriptive text to accompany your fundraising campaign. Include photos, introduce the team’s members, show your team’s character and make it interesting, inspiring and eye-catching. Show them your work, your vision and be clear on the reason the money will be used. You can even reward certain amount of donations (e.g., we sent out 3D printed keychains with our team’s name for every 100$ donation!)

8)Promote it! Let the world know you are raising money! Spread the news to your friends, family, old soccer mates or Facebook groups. Ideally, get in touch with groups of people affected by the problem you are planning to solve. Statistics show that if you raise 25% of your target in the first days, your chances of completing your target raise by up to 90%!!

9)Keep your backers updated! Send them a thank-you email, or even create a weekly or monthly newsletter to let them know about your team’s progress. It’s nice to show them that their money is well spent!

10)Don’t forget to attribute and acknowledge their contribution to your success in your wiki or social media.
